Biased random masked attention MAPPO algorithm for 
zero-shot scale generalization of multi-UAV air combat 
Mulai Tan 1 , Haocheng Sun2 , Huan Zhou1 , Zhihui Leng3 and Dali Ding 1 , * 
1 Aviation Engineering School, Air Force Engineering University, Xi’an 710038, China

Abstract 
With the wide application of uncrewed aerial vehicles (UAVs) in the military, multi-UAV cooperative air combat will become an impor- 
tant form of air combat. For the problem of multi-UAV swarm scale generalization, this paper proposes a biased random masked at- 
tention MAPPO (BRMA-MAPPO) algorithm, which achieves zero-shot scale generalization of multi-UAV swarms. Specifically, an entity 
observation encoder based on multi-head attention is introduced to address the variable number of multi-UAV swarms. Meanwhile, 
a biased random mask is proposed to enhance the performance at zero-shot scale generalization. The mask vector is generated by 
a mask vector generator network and applied to the attention matrix, masking non-essential entity information, thereby improving 
the generalization performance. The loss function of the mask vector generator network is constructed based on the information 
bottleneck theory, and the gradient of the mask network is derived. The simulation results show that the entity observation encoder 
proposed in this paper can help focus on key battlefield entity information and improve the training effect. The biased random mask 
proposed in this paper can mask non-important entity information, maintain focus on key entities when generalizing to larger sce- 
narios, and improve generalization effects. Compared with the existing zero-shot generalization multi-agent reinforcement learning 
algorithms, BRMA-MAPPO can significantly improve the generalization performance. In the 8V8 and 10V10 zero-shot generalization 
scenarios, the method proposed in this paper outperformed state-of-the-art algorithms by 12% and 15% in terms of winning rate, 
respectively. 
Keywords: attention, biased random mask, multi-UAV air combat, zero-shot generalization, MAPPO 
1. Introduction 
The existing UAVs have been applied in various fields due to their 
low risk, high cost performance and convenient movement, such 
as regional search and rescue (Lyu et al., 2023 ), relay communi- 
cation (Ahmed et al., 2020 ) and fire prevention and disaster relief 
(Khan et al., 2022 ). However, with the development of artificial in- 
telligence, the application of UAVs has been expanded, and UAVs 
will likely be used in unmanned autonomous air combat in the 
near future. For instance, the intelligent air combat competition 
organized by DARPA demonstrated the application of artificial in- 
telligence in air combat (Pope et al., 2023 ). Due to the limitations of 
individual UAVs, existing UAV confrontations often occur in clus- 
ters for autonomous cooperative confrontation (Xia et al., 2023 ). 
The most crucial part of autonomous cooperative confrontation is 
autonomous collaborative maneuvering decision-making, which 
provides maneuvering instructions and shooting instructions for 
UAVs. Although many scholars have been dedicated to solving the 
problem of multi-UAV cooperative confrontation (Liu et al., 2024 ; 
Yang et al., 2024 ), at present, the scale uncertainty caused by clus- 
ters poses challenges to maneuvering decision-making. 
The current cooperative maneuvering decision methods can 
be divided into two teams: hierarchical solution and direct solu- 
tion. Among them, the hierarchical solution method transforms 
the multi-UAV collaborative maneuvering decision-making prob- 
lem into a single-agent maneuvering decision-making problem 
through methods such as dynamic target allocation (Li et al., 2022 ; 
Minjian et al., 2020 ). The literature (Wang et al., 2024 ) conducted a 
systematic review of current air combat maneuvering decision- 
making methods, and pointed out that the current approaches to 
solving single-agent decision-making can mainly be classified as 
maneuvering decision-making methods based on mathematical 
solutions, maneuvering decision-making methods based on op- 
timization algorithms, and maneuvering decision-making meth- 
ods based on single-agent reinforcement learning. Among them, 
the maneuvering decision-making method based on mathemati- 
cal solution is directly solved by modeling as mathematical equa- 
tions, mainly including the influence graph method (Liu et al., 
2021 ; Lu et al., 2021 ) and the differential game method (Q. Li et 
al., 2022 ; Ramteke et al., 2022 ). 
The maneuvering decision method based on optimization al- 
gorithm is a method that uses intelligent optimization algorithms 
to solve the situation objective function by establishing the situa- 
tion objective function, which mainly includes genetic algorithm 
(Kang et al., 2021 ), differential evolution algorithm (Tan et al., 2025 )

method based on single-agent reinforcement learning models the

The direct solution method mainly generates collaborative ef- 
fects using multi-agent reinforcement learning methods. Cur- 
rently, various multi-agent reinforcement learning algorithms 
have been used to solve the problem of multi-UAV collaborative 
air combat. Literature (Yaoming et al., 2024 ) proposed Advantage 
Highlight Multi-Agent Proximal Policy Optimization (AHMAPPO). 
The algorithm addresses the issue of a small number of effec- 
tive samples in the multi-UAV collaborative beyond-visual-range 
air combat. Literature (Xu et al., 2025 ) proposes a method which 
using the attention decomposition value function to solve the 
credit allocation problem in multi-UAV air combat. An evolution- 
ary multi-agent reinforcement learning method is used to solve 
the reinforcement learning strategy loop problem in the litera- 
ture (B. Wang et al., 2024 ). Existing literature (Kong et al., 2023 ) has 
proposed a novel hierarchical maneuvering decision-making ar- 
chitecture tailored for close-range air combat scenarios involv- 
ing two unmanned aerial vehicles (UAVs), which effectively ad- 
dresses the challenge of coordinated air combat between dual 
UAVs. To further resolve poor coordination and suboptimal con- 
vergence in multi-UAVs coordinated air combat, literature (Yang et 
al., 2025 ) proposed the Sequential Decision Distributed Soft Actor- 
Critic (SD2AC) method. 
All of the above methods solve some aspects of the multi-UAV 
cooperative air combat problem to varying degrees. However, in 
actual multi-UAV swarm confrontations, the uncertainty of scale 
becomes a more significant challenge. When the agent faces a dif- 
ferent number of teammates and opponents that have never been 
trained before, there will be a marked decline in performance. It is 
unrealistic to acquire all common skills by training on all possible 
scale scenarios, as there are too many combinations of scales for 
both friendly and opposing parties. These reasons prompted us 
to conduct research on zero-shot migration in the out-of-domain 
(OOD) scenario. 
The existing methods have also attempted to address OOD 
scenarios. By taking the approach of appropriately expanding 
the Q or policy network via additional parameters, using in- 
ductive bias, it can address the zero-shot migration problem 
of OOD to a certain extent. Updet (Hu et al., 2021 ) can gener- 
alize to adversarial systems of different scales using the net- 
work structure of transformer. However, there is a significant per- 
formance degradation when generalizing from zero samples to 
larger scales. Refil (Iqbal et al., 2021 ) proposes Randomized Entity- 
wise Factorization for Imagined Learning which uses masks in 
attention-based models to achieve zero-shot generalization. ODIS 
(Zhang et al., 2022 ) addresses the issue of offline MARL general- 
ization by extracting coordination skills from known tasks and 
reusing them in unknown tasks to enhance generalization per- 
formance. However, this depends on the type of agent in the 
dynamic team composition. In literature (Chen et al., 2025 ), a 
method using attention-based observation coding and a cen- 
tralized critic was proposed, which can solve the multi-UAV 
air combat maneuvering decision-making problem of different 
scales. However, the paper did not conduct research on the per- 
formance when generalizing on a larger scale. All the above- 
mentioned literature has adopted the attention network struc- 
ture. However, the larger scale of the attention weight matrix 
during large-scale inference directly causes performance degra- 
dation. The FlickerFusion ( Koh et al., 2024 ) proposes to use the 
dynamic masking method to improve the performance when gen- 
eralizing from zero-shot to the out-of-domain scale. However, 
when this method is applied in the air combat environment, it 
is very likely to cause the discarding of key entity information, 
thereby resulting in performance degradation. 
To address the aforementioned issues, this paper proposes a bi- 
ased random mask attention multi-agent reinforcement learning 
method, which mainly includes an attention-based entity encoder 
and a biased random mask for entities. The main contributions of 
this article are as follows: 
(1) An attention-based entity observation encoder was pro- 
posed to encode entities in the MARL environment and use 
multi-head Attention to help agents focus on the most im- 
portant entities, thereby enhancing the agent’s battlefield 
situation focus and solving the problem of changes in the 
number of entities. 
(2) The biased random mask was proposed to improve the 
generalization performance of OOD. This paper proposes 
a mask vector generator network for generating mask vec- 
tors. The generated mask vectors mask non-important en- 
tity information by acting on the attention matrix, thereby 
improving generalization performance. 
(3) Training was conducted in 6V6 scenarios on a dynamic 
high-fidelity platform, and zero-shot generalization tests 
were carried out in 8V8 and 10V10 scenarios. The method 
proposed in this paper outperforms the existing OOD zero- 
shot generalization methods. Moreover, the method pro- 
posed in this paper can be integrated into the existing MLP 
backbone network.

ing and control systems (AWACS), thereby establishing a highly 
realistic air combat environment. The scene is shown in Figure 1 .

In the simulation, the UAV carries short-range AAMs. Since 
the simulation system incorporates a fire control system, mis- 
sile launch commands are generated according to the following 
rule: Missiles are launched when launch conditions are met and 
no recent missile launch events have targeted the same target. 
Given the short engagement range of short-range AAMs, to en- 
hance their hit probability, we propose a rule requiring missiles to 
be launched only after the enemy aircraft crosses the 3–9 line. The 
3–9 line refers to the rear hemisphere of the target aircraft. Geo- 
metrically, it corresponds to the angular range between 3 o’clock 
and 9 o’clock relative to the target’s heading direction, forming a 
180 fan-shaped area behind the target. Within this zone, the mis- 
sile can effectively lock onto the target’s infrared signature and 
avoid excessive maneuvering to intercept, thus significantly im- 
proving the hit probability. 
Moreover, the missile escape in this paper is realized by scripts, 
so the multi-agent reinforcement learning focuses on training 
of maneuvering confrontation, and the missile launch command 
and missile escape command are generated by the preset script.

The essence of the masking process is information compres-

carding redundant information, the model learns scale-invariant 
general features rather than over-relying on local patterns tied to 
a fixed number of entities. In this paper, the attention matrix scale

the number of entities leads to an expansion of the matrix scale. 
The masking process shields the redundant entities to prevent the 
model’s performance from deteriorating due to untrained param- 
eters. The biased random mask introduces moderate randomness. 
Through Gumbel-Softmax and entropy loss, the model adapts to 
changes in the entity distribution at different scales, enhancing 
its robustness to out-of-distribution scenarios.

3.4. Algorithm training and inference process 
The algorithm training framework is illustrated in Figure 6 . Our 
algorithm is trained in a 6V6 scenario, and a biased random mask 
is also employed during training to enable the network to adapt 
to variations in the number of masked entities. To preserve more 
information during training, the maximum number of masked 
UAVs for both friendly and enemy forces is set to 2. The mask 
vector, generated by the mask vector generator network, is fused 
with the random mask vector of friendly UAVs and the environ- 
mental death mask vector; this combined mask then acts on the 
multi-head attention mechanism of the entity observation en- 
coder, which outputs the observation features. Notably, the critic 
network adopts the same entity observation encoder but does 
not use the biased random mask—this  design aims to maintain 
the critic’s global observation capability, thereby enhancing its 
value assessment performance. Its pseudo-code is provided in Al-

4. Results and Discussion 
4.1. Simulation experiment parameter settings 
Using a single machine (AMD Ryzen 9 9900X CPU 4.5GHz, 24-core, 
one NVIDIA GeForce RTX 3090 24G GPU, 48GB RAM), all exper- 
iments were repeated three times with different random seeds. 
Key hyperparameter settings for the reinforcement learning algo- 
rithms are summarized in Table 3 , and simulation environment 
parameters are presented in Table 4 . During simulated confronta- 
tions, the blue team uniformly adopts a rule-based strategy. This 
strategy automatically performs target allocation and pursuit ac- 
cording to the battlefield situation, and executes missile evasion 
based on missile warning signals. Missiles are launched automat- 
ically once the launch conditions are satisfied. Upon reaching the 
maximum simulation duration, the red team is declared the win- 
ner if its number of surviving UAVs exceeds that of the blue team. 
4.2. Comparisons with SOTA methods 
The comparison algorithms include FlickerFusion, Refil, Updet, 
and MAPPO-Attention, QMIX and MADDPG. ReFil is a reinforce- 
ment learning algorithm proposed in 2021. It improves entity gen- 
eralization by decomposing random entity inputs and supporting 
auxiliary target training, while leveraging shared patterns across 
entity subsets. Updet is an outstanding zero-shot generalization 
algorithm based on transformer proposed in 2022, and Flicker- 
Fusion is an excellent zero-shot generalization algorithm based 
on dynamic random dropout proposed in 2024. MAPPO-Attention 
is obtained by introducing the entity observation encoder into 
the MAPPO algorithm. It is worth noting that MAPPO-Attention 
also uses the GRU network layer. QMIX and MADDPG are non- 
attention-based baseline algorithms developed in recent years. 
They achieves zero-shot generalization by defining a larger-scale 
observation dimension in a 6V6 training scenario. 
The comparison results are as follows: 
4.2.1. In-domain training (6V6) 
As shown in the reward and win-rate curves in Figure 7 , BRMA- 
MAPPO, MAPPO-Attention, and Refil exhibit similar reward trends, 
whereas FlickerFusion and Updet achieve slightly lower reward 
values by comparison. This may be related to the random masking 
during training process. Because random mask is essentially a reg- 
ularization technique, the training effect has declined due to the 
reduction of information. In the Win rate curve, the BRMA-MAPPO, 
MAPPO-Attention, Refil, FlickerFusion and Updet curves are sim- 
ilar, indicating that they have all been adequately trained in the 
6V6 scenario. The training performance of the QMIX and MADDPG 
algorithms was poor. This demonstrates that non-attention-based 
baseline methods achieve lower winning rates in multi-UAV coop- 
erative combat scenarios, as they do not employ attention mech- 
anisms. 
In order to further compare the training efficiency of BRMA- 
MAPPO with other algorithms, this paper compared the training 
time, single-step computation time and memory usage. 
From Table 5 , it can be seen that the training time of BRMA- 
MAPPO is longer than other algorithms except Updet. The main 
reason is that BRMA-MAPPO increases the training of the mask 
vector generator network parameters. Additionally, Updet uses 
the transformer, which makes the network relatively heavy and 
consumes a large amount of memory. In terms of single-step com- 
putation time, since both use reinforcement learning neural net- 
works to output actions, the single-step computation time is sim- 
ilar and can fully meet the real-time requirements of air com- 
bat games. The non-attention-based baseline algorithms such as 
QMIX and MADDPG in the table have less training time and mem- 
ory usage because they do not use the attention mechanism.

the biased random mask for entity observation masking. Because 
it has no attention matrix, the entity observations correspond- 
ing to the biased random mask are dropped. MAPPO-MLP that 
does not use the entity observation encoder and the biased ran-

scenario.

with all blue UAVs shot down. The red side launched a total of 31 
missiles throughout the engagement, whereas the blue side fired 
only 16 missiles. 
From the aforementioned confrontation processes, we observe 
that, owing to the stringent missile launch constraints in the air 
combat environment considered in this work, the battle outcome 
in large-scale engagements relies more heavily on the agent’s per- 
ception and understanding of battlefield situational awareness. 
Only by concentrating attention on high-threat enemies with po- 
sitional superiority and executing timely offensive or evasive ma- 
neuvers can the winning rate be effectively improved. Red UAVs 
can automatically and accurately search for targets based on real- 
time battlefield states, and maneuver to occupy favorable firing 
positions to satisfy missile launch conditions. Furthermore, the 
system maintains robust confrontation capability even when gen- 
eralized to larger-scale combat scenarios. 
5. Conclusions and Prospects 
This work proposes the BRMA-MAPPO algorithm, which integrates 
a biased random masking mechanism with an attention archi- 
tecture to address the zero-shot scale generalization problem in 
multi-UAV cooperative air combat. The model is trained in a 6v6 
scenario and directly generalized to 8v8 and 10v10 scenarios in 
a zero-shot manner. Based on ablation studies and attention dis- 
tribution visualization, the main conclusions are summarized as 
follows. 
First, the proposed entity observation encoder enables the 
agent to concentrate on critical battlefield entity information, 
thereby effectively enhancing training stability and performance. 
Second, the biased random mask mechanism filters out non- 
essential entity features, allowing the model to sustain focus on 
key entities when zero-shot generalized to larger-scale scenarios, 
thus significantly improving generalization performance. Com- 
pared with state-of-the-art zero-shot scale generalization meth- 
ods including FlickerFusion, Updet, and Refil, the proposed BRMA- 
MAPPO maintains a superior win rate under large-scale cross- 
scenario generalization. This work offers a novel perspective for 
the application of deep reinforcement learning in variable-scale 
collaborative confrontation scenarios. 
The proposed approach is well-suited for homogeneous UAV 
swarms and short-range air combat engagements. However, when 
extended to heterogeneous UAV swarms, it exhibits limited adapt- 
ability to diverse role characteristics, as the mask generation net- 
work does not differentiate between entity types. In future work, 
we will extend the framework to heterogeneous UAV swarms 
along two directions. The first is entity encoding adaptation, which 
incorporates UAV type embeddings into the entity observation 
encoder to distinguish functional roles such as reconnaissance 
and attack. The second is differentiated mask generation, which 
designs specialized mask generation networks for different UAV 
types: attack UAVs will prioritize masking non-target entities, 
while reconnaissance UAVs will retain more environmental and 
situational entities.
